Introduction:¶

In this lab we observed a capacitor as it charges and discharges in an RC circuit in different configurations. While discharging the capacitor, we calculated time constants of 4.8686 +/- 0.2119s, 12.4538 +/- 0.4184s and 18.4534 +/- 0.7803s for the 10, 20 and 30 μF capacitors in the circuit. While charging the capacitor, we calculated time constants of 5.6331 +/- 0.3529s, 12.0030 +/- 0.3847s, and 18.2158 +/- 0.7616s for the 10, 20 and 30 μF capacitors. We expected to obtain constants of 5.1s, 10.2s, and 15.3s, so our values are just within acceptable error ranges (although they're pushing it). When measuring the time constant using a function generator, we found a time constant of 0.00045s +/- .0001s and expected one of .000402s, so our value seems reasonable.

Procedure:¶

In the first part of this lab, we set up a circuit for charging the capacitor. We let the capacitor discharge fully and then flip the switch such that the capacitor bank begins charging. We took a video of the ammeter as the capacitors charged, and watched the video later to come up with measurements. We repeat this process for 3 different capacitor values 10, 20 and 30 μF. In the second part of this lab, we set up a circuit for discharging the capacitor. We let the capacitor charge fully, and then flipped the switch such that the capacitor banks began discharging. We took a video of the ammeter as the capacitors discharged, and watched the video later to come up with measurements. We repeated this process for 3 different capacitor values 10, 20 and 30 μF. In the third part of this lab, we used a smaller capacitor and set up a circuit such that the DC voltage came from a function generator instead of a DC power supply. We measured the resulting voltage drop across a small resistor using an analogue oscilloscope (as opposed to the digital one described in the lab). For 3 different frequencies between 100 and 150 Hz, we measured the change in voltage over a time period such that the drop in voltage was 1/e of the total drop since the time it takes to makes this drop is the value of the time constant Tao.

import numpy as np

def lsqfity(X, Y):
    """
    Copied from http://stackoverflow.com/questions/27764220/retrieve-the-standard-deviation-of-the-y-intercept
    Calculate a "MODEL-1" least squares fit.

    The line is fit by MINIMIZING the residuals in Y only.

    The equation of the line is:     Y = my * X + by.

    Equations are from Bevington & Robinson (1992)
    Data Reduction and Error Analysis for the Physical Sciences, 2nd Ed."
    pp: 104, 108-109, 199.

    Data are input and output as follows:

    my, by, ry, smy, sby = lsqfity(X,Y)
    X     =    x data (vector)
    Y     =    y data (vector)
    my    =    slope
    by    =    y-intercept
    ry    =    correlation coefficient
    smy   =    standard deviation of the slope
    sby   =    standard deviation of the y-intercept

    """

    X, Y = map(np.asanyarray, (X, Y))

    # Determine the size of the vector.
    n = len(X)

    # Calculate the sums.

    Sx = np.sum(X)
    Sy = np.sum(Y)
    Sx2 = np.sum(X ** 2)
    Sxy = np.sum(X * Y)
    Sy2 = np.sum(Y ** 2)

    # Calculate re-used expressions.
    num = n * Sxy - Sx * Sy
    den = n * Sx2 - Sx ** 2

    # Calculate my, by, ry, s2, smy and sby.
    my = num / den
    by = (Sx2 * Sy - Sx * Sxy) / den
    ry = num / (np.sqrt(den) * np.sqrt(n * Sy2 - Sy ** 2))

    diff = Y - by - my * X

    s2 = np.sum(diff * diff) / (n - 2)
    smy = np.sqrt(n * s2 / den)
    sby = np.sqrt(Sx2 * s2 / den)

    return my, by, ry, smy, sby

Analysis¶

Part 1¶

Yes, our calculated time constants barely agree within errors (when you account for possible 10% errors in the circuit components). Our results would have been far more accurate if we had a better way of measuring the current drop at different time points. Our current method was to film the drops and go in after the fact to measure them, but that caused even more pronounced parallax errors than we had seen before and wasn't super precise.

Part 2¶

Yes, our calculated time constant agrees within uncertainties, and with RC. There is no significant difference. The tolerance of circuit elements doesn't really matter for this part of the experiment, because our measurement of the .45mS time constant is so rough. If we had a digital scope with markers we could use to measure the 63% drop to find the time constant, we could have had great results. Our analog scopes, however, didn't have such features, so we really had to eyeball the drops. The magnitude and shape of the voltage across the resistor agree with what we'd expect based on our results from part 1. We see something logarithmic, with a sharp decline at the beginning and a slower decline toward the end. The voltage difference across the capacitor should start at 0 and increase to the voltage applied to it (in this case, from the function generator). This is simply the inverse of the voltage measured across the resistor after it (which is just a product of the current). The graph will look like the inverse of the ones earlier in this lab. The voltage drop across the capacitor + the voltage drop across the resistor must always add up to the voltage supplied by the power source (in our case, the function generator). It's constant. This is because of basic circuit laws. The internal resistance of the scope has little effect on the time constant of the circuit because it is placed in parallel with the 10k resistor. Electricity takes the path of least resistance, so almost all the current flows through the 10k resistor. If we had used the 1M resistor in the circuit, our time constant would be 100x larger. If we used a much higher frequency, our capacitor wouldn't have time to fully charge/discharge every time the voltage switched. We'd just see the very beginning of a logarithmic graph across the scope. Thus, the max frequency we could reasonably use would be 1/tao, and there's no real minimum (as long as we can observe 1 flip).
