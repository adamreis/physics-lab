Lab 9: AC Circuits¶

Introduction¶

In this lab we studied the frequencies and behavior of RLC circuits in alternating current driven by a sinusoidal voltage. We found that the resonance frequency for an RLC circuit was 3497.64 +/- 94.25 rads/sec, which falls within the expected resonance frequency of 3651.48 +/- 365.15 rads/sec. Using this same process, we calculated the inductance of a large, copper Inductor to be 30.78mH as opposed to the expected value of 36.65mH. When looking at the phase difference between the voltage over the resistor and the driving voltage, we found that our values were significantly different from the expected phase. When looking at the phase difference between the voltage over the inductor and the driving voltage, we found an angle of 78.62 degrees, which is close to the expected value of 90 degrees. We observed a phase difference between the voltage over the capacitor and the driving voltage of -86.11 degrees, which is even closer to the expected value of -90 degrees.

Procedure:¶

We set up the circuit as follows: We first measured the resonance of the circuit for 3 different resistances. We do this by measuring the voltage at 20 different frequencies. We then used a different inductor in the same circuit and measured the voltage at 20 different frequencies. Next we measured the phase difference between the driving voltage and the voltage over the resistor. Since one channel of the oscilloscope measured driving voltage, and another channel measured the voltage over resistor, we were able to measure the period and the difference between the peaks of the voltages using the cursor functionality on the oscilloscope. The last two sections of the lab were the same as above, however we set an especially high frequency (~10kHz) so that the inductor would be the dominant force causing the phase shift between driving voltage and voltage over the resistor. Similarly, we set an especially low frequency (~10Hz) so that the dominant force was the capacitance.

Phase of Driving Voltage and V_R¶

The measured and theoretical phase differences are printed above. They don't agree with each other, but they do all get smaller as they approach the resonance frequency and larger as they move away from it, which is to be expected.

Phase Difference of V (t) and the Capacitor/Inductor¶

The inductor voltage leads the driving voltage by 78.62 degrees and the capacitor voltage lags the driving voltage by 86.11 degrees. These are both pretty close to 90 degrees, which is the expected value. These former probably is statistically significant, but the latter isn't.

General Questions¶
  • At high frequencies, the inductor behaves as a coil of wire. As the current in the wire changes, magnetic flux of the wire changes as well. According to Lenzs Law, the induced current should be going in a direction that opposes the motion or change causing it. A high frequency will generate a signal that produces a strong flux, since the inductor is pushing back against the flow of the current to reduce it (hence the resistance). The inductor resists signals with high frequencies due to magnetic flux.
  • DC signals are generally blocked while high frequency AC signals are preferred for capacitors because DC signals fill the capacitor to its limit, which eventually blocks current flow. The rate of charge decreases exponentially, and it becomes harder to push in more charge.
  • On the other hand, high frequency AC signals continuously fluctuate, allowing the capacitor to charge and discharge with far less impedance than slower signals. Thus, the capacitor blocks DC signals and accepts high frequency AV signals.
  • We examined the signal across the resistor to indirectly examine the behavior of the capacitor and inductor since Vr can be read as it is proportional to the current, I. In addition, the resistor doesnt affect the resonant frequency, so that can be observed to indirectly examine the behavior of the capacitor and induction.
