Lab 10: Absorption of Beta and Gamma Rays¶

Introduction:¶

In this lab we observed the range of beta-particles from a Thallium-204 source to determine the endpoint energy of decay and calculated the absorption coefficient in lead of gamma radiation from a Cesium-137 source. We didn't go far enough to find the true endpoint, but at our highest measured thickness (26 mils = 0.06604cm) we measured an energy of decay of .64 MeV. We expect a maximum beta energy for Thallium-204 to be 0.765 MeV, with a range of 10.79 x 10^-4m, so our calculated values are close but slightly under. We calculated an absorption coefficient of gamma rays of .584 .0178 cm^-1 which gives us an energy of approx 1.7 .2 MeV. We expected the energy of the gamma rays to be .702 MeV, which corresponds to an absorption coefficient of about 1.15, so our observed values are significantly different.

Procedure:¶

First we calibrated the voltage of the Geiger counter by observing the counts off the Thallium-204 source at different voltages. We looked to see at what point increasing the voltage didnt increase the count by more than 10% over a 100v increase, and found this voltage to be at 860v. We then calculated the background radiation over 45s by removing the source and found it to be 1 count / second. We tried to minimize the error by covering keeping the sources as far away from the counter as possible. We maintained keeping the unused source as far as possible from the Geiger counter to ensure consistent background conditions. Next, keeping the Thallium-204 source, we measured the count over a 15 second period and varied the thickness of aluminum sheets between the source and the Geiger counter. We repeated this until the aluminum was thick enough that the Geiger counter only picked up background radiation. We repeated the same process using lead for the material with the Cesium-137 source.

def lsqfity(X, Y):
    """
    Copied from http://stackoverflow.com/questions/27764220/retrieve-the-standard-deviation-of-the-y-intercept
    Calculate a "MODEL-1" least squares fit.

    The line is fit by MINIMIZING the residuals in Y only.

    The equation of the line is:     Y = my * X + by.

    Equations are from Bevington & Robinson (1992)
    Data Reduction and Error Analysis for the Physical Sciences, 2nd Ed."
    pp: 104, 108-109, 199.

    Data are input and output as follows:

    my, by, ry, smy, sby = lsqfity(X,Y)
    X     =    x data (vector)
    Y     =    y data (vector)
    my    =    slope
    by    =    y-intercept
    ry    =    correlation coefficient
    smy   =    standard deviation of the slope
    sby   =    standard deviation of the y-intercept

    """

    X, Y = map(np.asanyarray, (X, Y))

    # Determine the size of the vector.
    n = len(X)

    # Calculate the sums.

    Sx = np.sum(X)
    Sy = np.sum(Y)
    Sx2 = np.sum(X ** 2)
    Sxy = np.sum(X * Y)
    Sy2 = np.sum(Y ** 2)

    # Calculate re-used expressions.
    num = n * Sxy - Sx * Sy
    den = n * Sx2 - Sx ** 2

    # Calculate my, by, ry, s2, smy and sby.
    my = num / den
    by = (Sx2 * Sy - Sx * Sxy) / den
    ry = num / (np.sqrt(den) * np.sqrt(n * Sy2 - Sy ** 2))

    diff = Y - by - my * X

    s2 = np.sum(diff * diff) / (n - 2)
    smy = np.sqrt(n * s2 / den)
    sby = np.sqrt(Sx2 * s2 / den)

    return my, by, ry, smy, sby

Analysis:¶

We didn't stack enough aluminum spacers to reach the maximum range of the measurement of Beta particles. We observed a maximum energy of .64 MeV, at our highest aluminum thickness, and expected .765 MeV an error of 16%. That's not too bad considering that we mistakenly didn't complete the entire experiment. With the Cesium-137 Source we see a larger discrepancy in the observed and expected value. We expect to see an energy of .702 MeV since 92% of the time theres an energy of .622 MeV and 1.17 MeV the other 8% of the time. This corresponds to an expected absorption coefficient of about 1.15 cm^-1 according to the graph in Figure 10.4. We observed an absorption coefficient of .584 .0178 cm^-1 which corresponds to an energy of approx 1.7 .2 MeV. The error in energy is 67% and the error in absorption coefficient is 66%. There are a few factors that can explain the discrepancy. First, the Geiger counter contains a gas and measures the ionization of that gas as the particles from the source strike the atoms in the gas. Thus it will be more accurate when those particles hitting the gas are larger, so the counts we get for beta radiation are more accurate than the counts we get for gamma radiation. Secondly we are looking up the energy with a graph and a ruler, which means we are subject to human error when taking this measurement. In this case, however, that error is insignificant in that it cannot explain a > 50% discrepancy. Finally we could again increase the accuracy of our results by conducting several trials at each width of lead between the source and the counter and using the mean and standard error to find a better fit. Another way to measure gamma rays is to use a gamma ray spectrometer. One type of gamma ray spectrometer uses a Scintillator and is composed of a charged crystal lattice with connected to a spectrometer such that when gamma rays hit the lattice, the spectrometer can measure the energy impact on the lattice and plot the number of photons at each frequency with greater accuracy than measuring the ionization of a gas being hit by gamma rays. This is a lower-cost method (in comparison to other gamma ray spectrometers) used by physicists today.

Conclusion:¶

We found that our observations about the maximum range and energy of beta particles was much more accurate than our observations of gamma radiation. We attribute this to the method of detecting the particles, because while Geiger counters are sufficient for beta radiation they have only 1% efficiency with gamma radiation. We also see that we could statistically increase the accuracy of our results by conducting several trials at each width of material.
